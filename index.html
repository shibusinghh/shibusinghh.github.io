About

“Most transformations don’t fail at the strategy. They fail at the last mile, where people decide whether to change.”

Organisations brokered my relationship with the world. I studied economics, political science and international relations in India before I ever touched a change plan, and it left me with a habit I have never shaken: reading every company the way you would read a country — its institutions, its incentives, its unwritten rules, and the distance between what its leaders announce and what its people actually experience.

A running thread through my career has been sitting in that distance. I started at Deloitte in 2020, guiding a 30,000-person workforce through the move to hybrid work, building the analytics that told leadership the truth about adoption, and designing the capability programme that made a generation of campus hires client-ready. That work taught me how organisations actually function, where they crack under change, and what it takes to make something new stick.

That conviction took me to EY, where I ran change on one of the largest HR technology programmes in Europe — a £30M+ Workday transformation across 100,000 people and eight agency brands — and to the other end of the spectrum, helping the Quality Council of India certify more than 2,000 small-business owners for whom a quality standard was not a slide but a livelihood. The pattern held everywhere: platforms do not change organisations; people do, one decision at a time.

Business school was a chance to pressure-test everything I thought I knew. At EDHEC I worked with Amadeus on how AI-native players could redraw travel distribution, with LVMH on what GenAI means for 75 maisons that prize human judgement, and with Lenovo on turning device returns into a circular business. With AI now rewriting how organisations decide, staff and serve, the questions I care about — adoption, governance, fairness — feel more urgent than ever.

That is what I want to keep building toward. Transformation that is genuinely adopted, honestly governed, and humane in how it treats the people it touches — change that closes the gap between go-live and changed behaviour, and earns its place in the working lives of the people who have to live with it.

Writing

Selected perspectives.

EU · In force from June 2026

The Pay Transparency Directive Is a Change Programme, Not a Reporting Exercise

From June 2026, employers across the EU must publish salary ranges, report gender pay gaps and justify differences against objective criteria. None of that is possible without knowing which roles constitute equal work — and most organisations genuinely do not know. I have seen what sits underneath: on one programme we found 4,200 job titles describing what turned out to be 580 roles.

My position: build the job architecture first, run the equity analytics second, and treat the levelling changes as a change programme in their own right. Titles and pay bands touch identity as much as data, and the organisations that treat this as a compliance filing will discover that the hard way.

EU · High-risk obligations 2026–2027

Govern to Adopt: What the AI Act Gets Right About Workplace AI

The EU AI Act classifies AI used in employment — recruitment, performance evaluation, task allocation — as high-risk, with obligations for human oversight and transparency. Many leaders read this as a brake on adoption. My experience designing GenAI adoption for LVMH’s 75 maisons suggests the opposite: people adopt AI faster when accountability is explicit, when they know which decisions remain theirs and who owns an override.

My position: design adoption and oversight as one system. The same operating model that produces your behavioural adoption KPIs should produce your oversight artefacts. Governance bolted on afterwards is compliance; governance designed in is trust.

EU & UK · Continuing obligation

Your HRIS Consolidation Is Also a Privacy Programme

Consolidating employee data across borders onto platforms like Workday raises data-minimisation, purpose-limitation and works-council questions that most programme plans discover too late. On WPP’s Workday programme I sat with legal and privacy stakeholders across eight agencies, running exception governance for legitimate local requirements so that privacy concerns lived inside the readiness process, not outside it.

My position: privacy is a trust issue before it is a legal one. Bring data-protection officers and employee representatives into the design early, and treat justified local exceptions as governance decisions — not resistance to be managed.

EU · Reporting phased from 2025

Audit-Grade People Data: CSRD’s Quiet Demand on HR

Corporate sustainability reporting now covers the company’s own workforce, and the disclosures carry assurance requirements. Pay equity, training, wellbeing — people data is becoming board material that auditors will test. Most HR functions are not ready, because reporting quality is an operating-model outcome: it depends on who owns the data, how definitions are governed, and whether the behaviours that generate the data are reliable.

My position: if the underlying behaviours are not governed, reporting becomes theatre. The analytics governance I ran for a 7,000-person service line, and the auditable impact reporting we designed at Lenovo, both start from the same discipline — make the behaviour reliable, and the numbers become defensible.
